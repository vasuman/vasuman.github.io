Mnemonics

1st Jan 2017

Breaking from my ususal pattern of posting about programming, I thought I’d write about (cue dramatic music) the hidden powers of the mind.

A friend recently recommended this book called Moonwalking with Einstein by Joshua Foer that dives into the world of competitive memorization. Records set in such contests include remembering and recalling 520 digits in sequence in a 5-minute period and the order of 1626 playing cards in an hour. Certainly, the contestants must be freaks of nature blessed with a photographic memory. Sorry to burst your bubble, but contrary to what popular media would have you believe, it’s higly unlikely that anybody has such abilities.

Then, what enables these people to perform such amazing feats of memory when I struggle to remember what I had for breakfast yesterday (it was Dosa, I think). Well, it turns out that you can accomplish pretty amazing things with just an average memory using the right techniques.

While most memory competitions include a variety of tasks (names and faces, playing cards and even poerty), for the rest of this post I’m going to focus specifically on the task of remebering long strings of digits in sequence. But, the general idea that I wish to convey is that there are methods to make seemingly impossible memorization tasks into tractable problems.

Chunking

Chunking is a psychological process that assists the human mind in storing and retrieving information. It works by grouping individual pieces of information into larger units that can be remembered and recalled together. This helps by effectively reducing the unique items that your mind need to remember. You may notice this happen subconciously when trying to memorize a telephone number, you generally split the number by grouping its component digits into semantically meaningful parts, like the country code, STD code, area code, etc. And then remebering these parts individually.

Major system

The Major system is a method that helps convert digits into consonant sounds in which each digit maps to one or more sounds.

Person-Object-Action system

The Person-Object-Action (POA) system associates each n-digit number with a person performing an action with an object. The n here is the chunk size (usually 2 or 3).
